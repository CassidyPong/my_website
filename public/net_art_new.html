Brief History

Not many people know the true story behind the Disney fairy-tales; mostly based off the Brothers Grimm stories (19th century, Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm), the original fairy-tales typically consisted of dark, gruesome themes in comparison to what we know today. Many (if not most) of the Brothers Grimm stories involve death, pain, gore, incest, etc. On the other hand, the Walt Disney versions are catered toward a younger, more innocent audience. Therefore the stories are more cheerful, light-hearted, optimistic, and full of happy-endings.

The time periods between the two are drastically different. Based off of German folklore, the Grimm brothers sought to preserve a collection of folk-tales that were spread orally. They intended the stories to be directed more toward an adult audience, addressing themes such as death, violence, sex, rape, etc. However, these stories were not widely accepted causing them to revise their stories, but not by much:ie.'mother' changed to 'stepmother' and references tosex were removed.Gradually, the fairy-tales became more conservative: violence and gory details were removed, appealing to adults who finally felt comfortable reading these stories to their children.

Artist's Statement

In this nonlinear narrative, I wanted to explore and bring into light the "darker side of things." Since most people do not know the original stories, I wanted to capitalize on the efficiency and convenience of the internet. First, I wanted to establish a clear difference between the Disney stories and the Grimm stories by using different colors, text, interactivity, and layout. For the Disney stories, I decided to create a interactive book where the viewer can flip actual pages to read through the story. On the other hand, I settled with simple text for the Grimm stories, but I included a "TL;DR" (aka too long, didn't read) on the right side for people who do not want to read the entire story since they are very, very long. All of the stories link to one another; one can jump from story to story allowing he/she to compare the differences between the Disney side and the Grimm side.

In total, there are twenty stories: Cinderella (Disney & Grimm), Rapunzel (Disney & Grimm), Hansel and Gretel (Disney & Grimm), Little Red Riding Hood (Disney & Grimm), Snow White and the Seven Dwarves (Disney & Grimm), Sleeping Beauty (Disney & Grimm), Princess and the Frog (Disney & Grimm), The Little Mermaid, Rumpelstiltskin, The Wolf and the Seven Young Kids, The Golden Bird, The Seven Ravens, and the Twelve Brothers. Though there are only twenty stories listed, there are many more Grimm stories including: Godfather Death, The Girl without Hands, The White Snake, The Dog and the Sparrow, Hans the Hedgehog, and many, many more. Personally, I find their stories very interesting and rather engaging. The formality in which the story is told also has its own characteristic, giving a different feel to the story as well.
